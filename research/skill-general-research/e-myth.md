# E-Myth Revisited — Research Summary

Source: "The E-Myth Revisited: Why Most Small Businesses Don't Work and What to Do About It" by Michael E. Gerber

## The Core Thesis

The "Entrepreneurial Myth" (E-Myth): the fatal assumption that someone who understands the technical work of a business can successfully run a business that does that work. A great baker doesn't automatically make a great bakery owner.

**Work ON the business, not IN the business.** Dedicate time to developing systems and processes that enable the business to operate autonomously.

## The Three Personalities

Every business owner contains three conflicting personalities:

1. **The Technician** — The doer. Lives in the present. "If you want it done right, do it yourself." Loves the work itself.
2. **The Manager** — The planner. Focuses on efficiency, order, predictability. Plots tactical action.
3. **The Entrepreneur** — The visionary. Sees the future. Defines the vision and closes the gap between now and where they want to be.

Most small businesses are started by Technicians having an "entrepreneurial seizure" — they're good at the work, so they think they should run a business doing the work. But running the business IS a different kind of work.

**Application to SimplyUI:** The developer using the skill is a Technician (good at building UI). The skill needs to temporarily make them think like an Entrepreneur (what's the vision?) and a Manager (what's the system?). The skill IS the system.

## The Franchise Prototype

The cornerstone of Gerber's philosophy. Pretend your business is the prototype for 5,000 more just like it. Design it so it can be replicated.

Key rules:
- **Systems-dependent, not people-dependent.** "The system runs the business. The people run the system."
- **Operated by people with the lowest possible level of skill.** If your model depends on highly skilled people, it's impossible to replicate.
- **Impeccable order.** All work documented in operations manuals.
- **Uniformly predictable service.** The customer gets the same result every time.

McDonald's example: Ray Kroc understood that the hamburger wasn't his product — McDonald's was. The SYSTEM was the product.

**Application to SimplyUI:** The skill itself is the franchise prototype. It should produce consistent, high-quality briefs regardless of:
- Which UI problem is brought to it
- How articulate the developer is about design
- How complex or simple the feature is

The same process, the same questions, the same output format. Every time.

## Innovation → Quantification → Orchestration

The three-step cycle for building systems:

1. **Innovation** — Constantly ask "What is the best way to do this?" Try new approaches.
2. **Quantification** — Measure the impact. Without numbers, you can't know where you are.
3. **Orchestration** — Once you find what works, BAKE IT IN. Eliminate discretion at the operating level. The successful change becomes the new standard.

"Orchestration is the elimination of discretion, or choice, at the operating level of your business."

**Application to SimplyUI:** The skill has been through Innovation (building the framework). Now it needs Orchestration — bake in what works, remove the moments where Claude has to improvise questions. Fixed extraction system, not "ask these kinds of questions."

## The Lowest Skill Level Principle

"Your system should not depend on highly skilled people — they will become the bane of your existence."

Design the system so that people with minimal skill can operate it and produce consistent results. The system compensates for skill gaps.

**Application to SimplyUI:** The skill should work for someone who has never thought about UI design. If the questions require design thinking to answer well, the system is people-dependent. The questions should be concrete enough that anyone can answer them: "What does the user tap first?" not "What's the information hierarchy?"

## Operations Manuals = Checklists

An operations manual is a series of checklists — the authoritative guidebook for how things are done. Every position gets one.

Key properties:
- Step-by-step instructions
- Clarify roles and responsibilities
- Can be referred to for instruction, analysis, and improvement

**Application to SimplyUI:** The brief IS the operations manual for building the feature. It should be a checklist that any builder (human or AI) can follow without needing to understand the design thinking that produced it.

## Soft Systems for Capturing Ideas

Gerber identifies three types of systems:
- **Hard Systems** — Physical things (tools, equipment)
- **Soft Systems** — Ideas and processes (how to do things)
- **Information Systems** — Data and feedback

Soft Systems are the most important — they capture HOW the business runs at its best. By documenting processes, you generate consistency.

**Application to SimplyUI:** The conversation between the skill and the developer IS a Soft System for extracting design intent. It needs to be documented and repeatable, not improvised.

## Creativity vs. Systemization

Gerber directly addresses the concern that systems kill creativity: "Routine will free people to be creative where it matters."

The system handles the predictable parts. Creativity is reserved for the parts that actually need it. Structure facilitates creativity — it doesn't kill it.

**Application to SimplyUI:** The systematic extraction (fixed questions, fixed process) frees the developer to be creative about the ANSWERS. They're not spending mental energy figuring out what to think about — the system tells them what to think about.
